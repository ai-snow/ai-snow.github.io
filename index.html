The Ultimate Rabbit Hole

Language translation is not a one-to-one function. Some are more equipt to encode certain concepts more effectively.

There are languages for music, conversation, mathematics, and computation, to name a few high-level categories.

But, even within these categories are languages more adept at certain domain applications.

The design of a language is directly related to what type of problems it can solve and which concepts are expressable.

Does a population know a concept if it doesn't have a way of expressing it with language?

The standard digital computer has a language of 0s and 1s which can be ordered in sequence to be interpreted as operations.

Is there a language, or a set of languages, that when combined can describe everything, and not only from a physical perspective?

This is not about predictability.

This is about expressability.

In the mature Information Age, do we have the right languages for the post-Information Age?

I'd say no, not even close.

What's the problem with what we have?

Inconsistency, for one.

Information needs to be consistent.

Language without consistency has no meaning.

Can language be logically consistent and fully expressive at the same time?

The Earth is approximately a sphere.

How do we know this statement is consistent with our view of reality?

Most people know it wasn't considered true a few hundred years ago.

What makes it considered true now?

Does "Earth" still refer to the same object/subject?

Does "sphere" still refer to a surface which every point on it is equidistant from the origin or a frame of reference?

Does "approximately" still refer to the difference between two things being minimal in the current frame of reference?

Can language be perpetually consistent?

Or, rather, can a language be designed to be perpetually consistent?

If not perpetually, can it be consistent at a given point in time, and can that consistency be understood consistently into the future, even though the language in the future may be different?

Can we use a mathematical framework that tags timestamps to assignments?

Can natural language be constructed in the same way?

$$\text{The Earth is approximately a sphere} = true$$

$$f(now, \text{The Earth is approximately a sphere}) \rightarrow true$$

Where \(f(t, statement)\) returns whether or not a statement is consistent at time \(t\).

Why am I concerned with consistency and not truth?

Truth is very hard to show, especially through language.

At a minimum, local consistency is necessary for language to mean anything.

Without consistency, truth cannot be expressed or communicated.

If I have my own personal language that no other person understands, how do I communicate with others?

Knowledge becomes siloed.

There may be groups of people debating different topics, but if the underlying language is inconsistent between groups, what is the point of debate?

How do we let the future know what was the true definitions of things/concepts?

How can a decentralized system continue if communication between individuals is not consistent?

Changes in communication protocol has to diffuse through the system, but if everyone is diffusing their own protocol, the system dies eventually.

Humans are not inherently centrally controlled; central control is imposed on us, whether that is good or bad, whatever your opinion.

We still communicate and behave in a decentralized manner.

Central control mechanisms only influence our decentralized behavior.

How can central control mechanisms communicate consistently to everyone if everyone is not being consistent between themselves?

“Tradition is not the worship of ashes, but the preservation of fire.” - Gustav Mahler

Language is a tradition.

Changes to a tradition cannot be so big that they snuff out the "fire" of the tradition.

New logs need to be added to a fire for it to be sustained, but if those logs are too big (among other things), that fire will not be sustained.

This is a rather important concept to grasp.

To sustain a "fire" or tradition, there has to be both conservatism and liberalism (classical).

The liberal says "hey, we need to add this log or the fire will go out soon."

The conservative says "well, that log will snuff out the fire, I think this other log will be more suitable."

If liberals and conservatives can't communicate, the fire is not maintained properly and everyone is left with nothing.

That is why consistency in language is the most important concept in a decentralized system, especially a dynamic one.

Let's make a psychological analogy here.

There's the theory of the ego and the superego.

The superego is sometimes described as the "inner-critic".

Psychologists say we need to tame this inner identity.

The ego needs to be developed in a healthy way, but those growing up in a traumatic household tend to not develop an ego, they say, and are controlled by the superego entirely.

Here's the analogy:

The extremes on the ends (extreme-conservatism, extreme-liberalism) are like the superegos of the two groups.

Whereas the non-extremists are more like the egos of the groups.

If the two camps are themselves a form of consciousness (group-think), then taming the extremes is paramount.

If their superegos are given control and primary communication filtering, then it is as though our collective consciousnesses have been traumatized.

Can such a state of affairs be alleviated through group-on-group discourse?

Probably not. There needs to be another collective consciousness that comes along to mediate them and bring the egos back into power.

Let's eventually discuss this "other consciousness," but less move to something else that is a problem.

Language today is not continuous, as well.

At a maximum, it is piecewise coninuous with the addition of mathematics.

Biologically speaking, intuitively distinguishing objects into specific characteristics has an evolutionary advantage in a primitive society.

If you are male looking to reproduce, it is fairly advantageous to identify a reproductive partner as female.

This is a very valid and useful use of discrete thinking.

Decisions are usually applied in definite categorical fashion.

You either do something or you don't.

But, sometimes we can partially do something, in some sense.

Stock traders can make fuzzy-like decisions with their investments in that they can put different percentages of their wealth in different assets.

Portfolios can be described in ways such as 50% in category A and 50% in category B.

Although we 100% made trades of the stock A and stock B, we participate in both price movements in a partial fashion.

This hints at a way of making decisions that are less binary (yes/no).

But it is still discrete categories.

In the stock case, there are discrete stocks, so this makes sense; there's not a continuous spectrum of stocks to choose from.

How many concepts in the real world can be categorized continuously?

Well, there's the \(time\) category, which is continuous.

Our exact moment in birth is a timestamp within the continuous spectrum of time.

Temporal events tend to be within a continous time spectrum (although this assumes there is not a fundamental smallest time-step, but let's assume time is indeed continuous).

Color is a continous spectrum that we rarely utilize in a continuous fashion (in a daily usage reference).

Context usually decides how specific we are, but we don't go around saying the wavelengths of objects.

And for fairly good reason: we biologically cannot output this information biologically.

Our input of color information is virtually continuous, but our output, or are way of sharing color information is mostly not continuous.

What I mean by "mostly" is that we could trial-and-error our way using paints or tools to get to a close representation in a continuous spectrum and tell people how to replicate this color using our findings.

How important the precision is depends on the situation, but in everyday conversation we simply do not have time to bother constructing a continuous representation.

We perform a clustering process in our brains to facilitate efficient communication.

In many respects, this clustering is quite rational.

The question to ask is how the loss in precision is compounded over time.

Metaphorically speaking, if the compound factor is \(1\), then there is no need to be concerned.

But any compound factor greater than \(1\) yields chaotic behavior eventually within interactions of other compounding factors.
