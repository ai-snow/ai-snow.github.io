Language today is not continuous, as well.

Biologically speaking, intuitively distinguishing objects into specific characteristics has an evolutionary advantage in a primitive society.

If you are male looking to reproduce, it is fairly advantageous to identify a reproductive partner as female.

This is a very valid and useful use of discrete thinking.

Decisions are usually applied in definite categorical fashion.

You either do something or you don't.

But, sometimes we can partially do something, in some sense.

Stock traders can make fuzzy-like decisions with their investments in that they can put different percentages of their wealth in different assets.

Portfolios can be described in ways such as 50% in category A and 50% in category B.

Although we 100% made trades of the stock A and stock B, we participate in both price movements in a partial fashion.

This hints at a way of making decisions that are less binary (yes/no).

But it is still discrete categories.

In the stock case, there are discrete stocks, so this makes sense; there's not a continuous spectrum of stocks to choose from.

How many concepts in the real world can be categorized continuously?

Well, there's the \(time\) category, which is continuous.

Our exact moment in birth is a timestamp within the continuous spectrum of time.

Temporal events tend to be within a continous time spectrum (although this assumes there is not a fundamental smallest time-step, but let's assume time is indeed continuous).

Color is a continous spectrum that we rarely utilize in a continuous fashion (in a daily usage reference).

Context usually decides how specific we are, but we don't go around saying the wavelengths of objects.

And for fairly good reason: we biologically cannot output this information biologically.

Our input of color information is virtually continuous, but our output, or are way of sharing color information is mostly not continuous.

What I mean by "mostly" is that we could trial-and-error our way using paints or tools to get to a close representation in a continuous spectrum and tell people how to replicate this color using our findings.
